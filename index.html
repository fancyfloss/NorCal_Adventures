The SF Food Guide
California originals · Local legends · Things you can't get in the UK

A Note for CG & Bella — Coming from the UK

  • California cuisine was literally invented here — farm-to-table, seasonal menus, local sourcing all started in the Bay Area (Alice Waters at Chez Panisse, Berkeley)
  • SF has more Michelin-starred restaurants per capita than almost anywhere in the US — but the best meals are often at non-starred locals
  • Dungeness crab season runs Nov–June — they will be in full season during this visit. Order it everywhere.
  • The Mission burrito is a SF invention — larger, more stuffed, and fundamentally different from anything called a burrito in the UK
  • Sourdough here isn't like sourdough anywhere else — SF's fog and bacteria create a tang that literally cannot be replicated outside this microclimate
  • Reservation strategy: Book Cotogna, Zuni Café, and Bix at least 2 weeks out. Many others are walk-in friendly.
⭐ The Essential SF Restaurants — Book These Now
Jackson Sq · Must Book
Cotogna
The single best dinner recommendation in this guide. Chef Michael Tusk's rustic Italian on Pacific Ave in Jackson Square — wood-burning oven, house-made pasta, spit-roasted meats and fish, produce from their own farm in Bolinas. Menu changes daily. The singular raviolo (face-sized, egg yolk oozing into brown butter) is legendary. Michelin-recognized. Book 30 days out on SevenRooms or try the bar walk-in.
Hayes Valley · Must Book
Zuni Café
SF institution since 1979. The roasted chicken for two (brined 24 hours, cooked in a wood-fired oven, served with warm bread salad) is one of the most famous dishes in California food history. Order it. Also: impeccable Caesar salad at the zinc bar, raw oysters, excellent cocktails. A pillar of California cuisine.
Mission · Walk-in Friendly
Tartine Bakery
The bakery that changed American bread culture. Chad Robertson's country loaf comes out at 5pm daily — people queue. The morning bun (croissant dough, orange zest, cinnamon sugar) is one of SF's iconic foods. 18th & Guerrero. Get there before it's gone.
FiDi · Book Ahead
Bix
Hidden in Gold Alley off Kearny — a glamorous 1930s supper club with live jazz most nights. The steak tartare is extraordinary. Exceptional cocktails. This is date-night SF at its most atmospheric and only locals seem to know about it.
NOPA · Late Night
Nopa
Open until midnight. Wood-fired everything. The pork chop, the burger at the bar, the communal table energy — this is what quintessential SF restaurant culture looks like. Busy and loud and perfect. Try the bar burger after 10pm — one of the city's cult late-night items.
Mission · Book Ahead
Flour + Water
The pasta restaurant that rivals Cotogna. Handmade pasta from California grains, incredibly seasonal menu. The tagliatelle with Bolognese and the tasting menu format are both outstanding. On Valencia Street — walk the neighborhood after.
🦀 Seafood — California's Greatest Gift
Richmond · Local Secret
PPQ Dungeness Island
Garlic noodles and Dungeness crab cracked tableside. Messy, communal, glorious. This is the SF crab ritual. Clement Street in the Richmond. A local institution that visitors almost never find.
Embarcadero
Waterbar
Floor-to-ceiling cylindrical fish tanks, Bay Bridge views, raw bar with the best oyster selection in SF. Hog Island oysters, local Dungeness crab, sea urchin. Happy hour is one of the best deals on the waterfront.
Ferry Building
Hog Island Oyster Co. (Ferry Building)
Hog Island's Tomales Bay oysters — raised 70 miles north in pristine tidal waters — served raw, BBQ, or Rockefeller. The Ferry Building location has direct bay views. For the full farm experience, make the drive to Marshall (see Sonoma tab).
Outer Sunset
Outerlands
Warm beach-cottage restaurant two blocks from Ocean Beach. Dutch pancakes, housemade bread, excellent brunch. The seasonal menu changes with what's local. One of SF's most genuinely cozy spots.
North Beach
The Italian Homemade Company
Simple, honest, extraordinary pasta made fresh daily. Cacio e pepe, carbonara, pesto genovese. Counter service, no frills, always a line. The best quick pasta in the city.
Fisherman's Wharf
Alioto's Restaurant
SF's oldest family restaurant (1925) on the wharf. Cioppino (the SF invention — tomato-wine seafood stew) is the dish. Crab, clams, mussels, shrimp, calamari in one bowl. The quintessential SF seafood experience.
🌮 California Originals — Dishes Born Here
Mission
La Taqueria — The Original Mission Burrito
James Beard award-winning taqueria. The Mission burrito (foil-wrapped, rice-optional, perfect proportion) was essentially invented in this neighborhood. La Taqueria on Mission Street is the gold standard — carnitas or carne asada, no rice, extra guac.
Mission
El Combre
Authentic Mission-style Mexican, one block from Dolores Park. The carnitas burrito is the size of a forearm. Order the horchata alongside. Classic, no-frills, essential.
Mission
La Palma Mexicatessen
Family-run since 1953. They make tortillas by hand in-store — you can watch them. Get the carnitas tamale and fresh chips. 24th & Florida. An SF food landmark.
Mission
Bi-Rite Creamery
Salted caramel and honey lavender ice cream — flavors that became nationally influential when Bi-Rite opened in 2006. The line on 18th Street is always worth it. Across from Dolores Park.
Wharf
Boudin — SF Sourdough
The sourdough starter here dates to 1849. SF's famous tang (from local Lactobacillus sanfranciscensis — a bacterium that only thrives in this bay microclimate) cannot be replicated anywhere else in the world. The clam chowder bread bowl is mandatory.
Richmond
Burma Love
Burmese cuisine that SF locals are evangelical about. Tea leaf salad (fermented tea leaves, fried garlic, tomatoes, sunflower seeds) is unlike anything available in the UK. Rainbow salad, coconut chicken noodles. One of the most essential SF restaurant experiences.
🍜 The Local Neighborhood Dining Scene
Chinatown
Hang Ah Tea Room
Oldest dim sum restaurant in the US (est. 1920), tucked in Pagoda Place alley. Hand-rolled har gow, pork siu mai, egg tarts. A piece of living culinary history.

North Beach
Molinari Deli
North Beach Italian deli since 1896. The muffuletta and Italian combo sandwich (soppressata, mortadella, provolone, giardiniera on Dutch crunch bread) is extraordinary. Get it to go and eat in Washington Square Park.
Mission
Mission Chinese Food
New American-Chinese that rewrote the rulebook. Salt cod fried rice, mapo tofu with numbing Sichuan chili, salt and pepper crab. Bold, irreverent, creative. Nothing like Chinese food anywhere in the UK.
Noe Valley
Contigo
Catalan small plates in a neighborhood setting. Patatas bravas, pan con tomate, excellent Ibérico ham. One of the best tapas experiences in the city, zero pretension.
Sunset
Thanh Long
Since 1971 — one of the first Vietnamese restaurants in the US. The garlic noodles here reportedly influenced a young Wolfgang Puck. The roasted crab with garlic noodles is the dish. Call ahead.
Inner Sunset
Izakaya Rintaro
Japanese izakaya in a beautiful cedar-wood interior built by SF craftsmen. The skewers, house-made tofu, and sake selection are exceptional. One of the finest Japanese restaurants in the city.
Ferry Building
Slanted Door
Modern Vietnamese that put SF on the national food map. Charles Phan's shaking beef, cellophane noodles with Dungeness crab, and green papaya salad are all exceptional. One of SF's most celebrated restaurants.
☕ Bakeries, Coffee & SF Sweets
Richmond
Arsicault Bakery
Named best croissant in America by Bon Appétit. Arguello & Cabrillo in the Richmond. Go before 10am — they run out. Butter croissant, almond croissant, and the plain are all essential.
Nob Hill
Butter & Crumble
SF's most creative pastry shop. Near Cable Car Museum. Seasonal flavors, incredible laminated doughs. Go with an empty stomach and order one of everything.
North Beach
Café Trieste
SF's oldest espresso bar, open since 1956. Francis Ford Coppola wrote The Godfather screenplay at a table here. Excellent espresso, Italian pastries, opera on Saturday afternoons from the family. Go for the history alone.
SoMa
Sightglass Coffee
SF's most beautiful coffee space — soaring ceilings, industrial warehouse, exceptional single-origins. One of the defining SF roasters alongside Ritual and Blue Bottle.
Japantown
Benkyodo
The Nishikawa family has been making mochi and sembei crackers at this tiny Japantown shop since 1906. The mochi is hand-rolled fresh. One of the last authentic family mochi shops in the US.
Chinatown
Golden Gate Fortune Cookie Factory
Ross Alley, Chinatown — still making fortune cookies by hand on original 1950s presses. $.75 for a bag, and you can watch them fold. Not a tourist trap — a genuine surviving cottage industry.
Mission
Bi-Rite Market
The market that supplies the Mission's best food. Incredible cheese counter, local charcuterie, prepared foods. Build the perfect Dolores Park picnic here — grab wine, cheese, and their famous fried chicken before heading to the park.
Ghirardelli Sq
Ghirardelli Chocolate Sundae
The original hot fudge sundae — Ghirardelli invented it in 1962. Touristy and genuinely delicious. The Earthquake Sundae (eight scoops) is the stuff of legend. A SF rite of passage.
🌾 Saturday Farmers Markets — The Real California Food Experience

Ferry Building Farmers Market
The best farmers market in California. Over 100 local vendors. The chilaquiles vendor has a line before 9am. Get strawberries (first of the season in April), fresh cheese, bread, oysters. This is California food culture at its peak — and nothing like a UK market.
Saturday · 8am–1pm
Noe Valley Saturday Farmers Market
24th Street in Noe Valley. Smaller, calmer, very local. Great for a neighborhood morning — cheese from Cowgirl Creamery, bread from Acme, fresh produce. Pairs with coffee at one of the nearby cafés.
Year-Round
Cowgirl Creamery (Ferry Building)
Point Reyes Marin County farmstead cheese made from single-herd organic milk. The Mt. Tam triple-cream and Red Hawk washed-rind are nationally celebrated. Buy a wedge and eat it immediately. This is what California cheese culture looks like.
🥟 Dim Sum — The Bay's Unofficial Sunday Religion
Richmond · Weekend
Ton Kiang ⭐
The gold standard for weekend dim sum in SF. Hakka-style, meticulously made, always fresh. The turnip cake, cheung fun, and char siu bao are benchmarks. Expect a wait — arrive by 10am Saturday or Sunday. The experience of dim sum in a packed, loud, warm dining room is something they simply don't have in the UK.
Sunset District
Dragon Beaux
Elevated, modern dim sum with gorgeous presentation and creative fillings. Instagrammable soup dumplings and rainbow har gow. Book ahead — this one is extremely popular and seats go fast on weekends.
Chinatown
Good Mong Kok Bakery
The most legendary hole-in-the-wall bakery in SF's Chinatown. A line of regulars every single morning. Pork and shrimp dumplings, cha siu bao, taro puffs, egg tarts — all made fresh and priced for the neighborhood. No frills, perfect everything.
Sunset · Weekend Only
Yank Sing
SF institution for decades. Cart dim sum in a grand dining room, with classics done impeccably — steamed pork ribs, sesame balls, sticky rice in lotus leaf. The gold standard for occasion dim sum. Book in advance on weekends.
🍜 The Asian Diaspora Table — The Bay Area's Greatest Gift to the World

Why this matters

The San Francisco Bay Area has the largest and most diverse concentration of Asian-American communities outside Asia itself. The Vietnamese in the Tenderloin (refugees arrived after 1975), the Filipino diaspora across SoMa and Daly City, the Chinese communities in multiple Chinatowns, the Japanese Americans of Japantown, the Koreans in Oakland's KONO district, the South Asians of the South Bay — this is a food culture unlike anywhere else in the world, and most of it is available for the price of a bowl of noodles. This is the food that Anthony Bourdain came here specifically to eat. Don't miss it.

🇵🇭 Filipino — The Bay's Soul Food
Fisherman's Wharf · Fine Dining
ABACÁ ⭐
The finest Filipino restaurant in California — full stop. Chef Francis Ang's contemporary Filipino-Californian cooking at the Kimpton Alton Hotel. Ube-inspired specialties, lumpia made with local California ingredients, sisig fried rice, buko pie and bibingka from the attached panaderia bakery. This is what Filipino food can become in the right hands. Book in advance — it fills quickly.
SoMa Pilipinas
Señor Sisig
The Filipino-Mexican fusion concept born in SF that made the Mission burrito its canvas. Sisig tacos, sisig burritos, rice bowls — pork and chicken cooked in the citrusy, crispy Filipino style. A Bay Area original. Multiple locations; the Mission on Valencia is the OG.
Tenderloin · Authentic
Kusina Ni Tess
237 Ellis St. Authentic home-style Filipino comfort food from a small town in the Philippines. Adobo, tapsilog (tapa/sinangag/itlog), laing, dinuguan, sinigang — all cooked like someone's nanay made it. Breakfast served all day. Inexpensive, personal, real.
Daly City · Turo-Turo
Chibog (Daly City)
The definitive turo-turo experience (point-point — you point at what you want behind the glass). Sizzling sisig with egg on top, chicken adobo, halo-halo. Daly City is the unofficial capital of Filipino America — a 15-minute BART ride from SF and worth every minute of the trip.
Mission · Brunch
Alnico (1050 Valencia)
Filipino-American brunch spot on Valencia with photo-worthy plates and genuinely delicious food. Ube hotcakes, niloco mo ako, Filipino community at the center of the experience. The place that shows how Filipino-American culture evolves without losing its heart.
Special Occasion
Restaurant Naides
One of SF's rare Filipino fine-dining tasting menu experiences. Cemented itself as the go-to for celebrations. The tasting menu traces the archipelago through modern technique — Filipino cuisine treated with the same reverence as French or Japanese.
🇻🇳 Vietnamese — The Tenderloin & Beyond
Tenderloin · Legacy
Turtle Tower ⭐ (220 California St)
Northern Vietnamese pho from the restaurant that put Hanoi-style broth on the SF map — the clear, fragrant, intensely savory version that's nothing like the southern bowls most Westerners know. Bún thang (rice vermicelli with chicken, egg, and pork), pandan-infused waffle to finish. Returned to the Financial District in 2025 after closing. A full-circle SF story.
Richmond · Institution
Crustacean ⭐ (195 Pine St)
The An family's landmark. Chef Helene An's secret-kitchen garlic noodles — arguably the single most influential dish in Bay Area food history, invented around 1978 and still made behind closed doors in a kitchen-within-a-kitchen. The roasted Dungeness crab with garlic noodles is the order. Moved to a grand new FiDi location in late 2025.
Tenderloin · Best Bánh Mì
Saigon Sandwich
The best bánh mì in SF — full stop. Tenderloin counter service, always a line at lunch. Get the classic: pâté, pork, pickled daikon and carrots, jalapeño, cilantro on a perfect baguette. Under $6. One of the best sandwiches in the world at any price.
Tenderloin · Noodle Shop
Hai Ky Mi Gia
Teochew noodle house — the cuisine of ethnic Chinese Vietnamese communities. The thick egg noodles topped with braised duck leg or char siu are spectacular. Order dry noodles (khô) with soup on the side. A Tenderloin institution at very reasonable prices.
Richmond
Lily on Clement
Beloved Inner Richmond Vietnamese restaurant on the restaurant row of Clement Street. The garlic noodles, hanoi BBQ platter, and whole fried fish are the signatures. The whole street of Clement between 1st and 9th Ave is a walking Vietnamese/Chinese/Korean/Russian food tour — best street for grazing in SF.
Inner Richmond
Y&Y Vietnamese Cuisine (Clement St)
Beloved neighborhood pho institution. Huge portions, authentic broths, very reasonable prices. The bún bò Huế (spicy Central Vietnamese beef noodle soup) is one of the best versions in the city. Pairs perfectly with a walk along Clement Street's Vietnamese, Chinese, and Korean shops.
🌮 Latin — Mexican & Beyond
Mission · James Beard
La Taqueria ⭐ (2889 Mission St)
The James Beard America's Classic award winner and birthplace of the Mission burrito style. The carne asada or carnitas burrito — no rice, just meat, beans, cheese, salsa — is a philosophical statement about purity. The quesadilla is also exceptional. Lines every day; worth every minute. This is the ur-burrito.
Mission
La Palma Mexicatessen
The tortilla factory of the Mission since 1953. They make tortillas and masa fresh all day — you can buy them hot off the press. Pupusas, tamales, handmade tortillas with carnitas. The salsas are some of the finest in the city. A living piece of Mission District heritage.
Mission · Weekend Brunch
El Combre
The Mission's local secret for weekend Mexican brunch. Huevos rancheros, chilaquiles, tamales — all from recipes that go back generations. No tourist markup, no pretension. A neighborhood spot that's been feeding the Mission for decades. Busy on weekends; arrive early.
Mission
Gracias Madre

🍛 Indian — The South Bay Pilgrimage

Why go to the South Bay for Indian food

Sunnyvale, Santa Clara, Fremont, and San Jose have the highest concentration of Indian immigrants in the US outside of New Jersey — and consequently, some of the finest Indian regional cooking in the world outside India itself. South Indian dosas, Hyderabadi biryani, Gujarati thali, Punjabi dal makhani: all here, all made for the diaspora by the diaspora, not for tourists. Easy 45-min Caltrain ride from SF.

Sunnyvale · South Indian
Dosa Place / Chaat Bhavan ⭐
Sunnyvale's legendary vegetarian South Indian restaurants — the dosas (crispy fermented rice-and-lentil crepes, taller than your arm span) come with sambhar and three different chutneys. The masala dosa with potato filling is one of the great breakfasts of the world. Very inexpensive, very authentic, always packed with Indian families — the highest possible endorsement.
Mountain View · Upscale
Sakoon (Mountain View)
The most acclaimed upscale Indian restaurant in the South Bay. Butter chicken and saag paneer done with refinement, alongside specialties like egg curry and bread pakora. Beautiful interior, great weekend buffet. The kind of Indian food that makes you reassess everything you thought you knew.
San Jose · Mughlai
Amber India (Santana Row)
Upscale modern Indian in San Jose's chic Santana Row. Beautifully presented curries, excellent tandoori section, strong cocktail program. The best option for a more formal Indian dinner without driving to Sunnyvale.
Sunnyvale · Chaat
Chaat House
The destination for chaat — Indian street snacks that are an explosion of textures and flavors: pani puri, bhel puri, dahi papdi, sev puri. All made fresh to order. This is the street food of Mumbai and Delhi, available on a strip mall in Sunnyvale. Mind-bending.
🥷 Hole-in-the-Wall SF — The Local's Hit List

The anti-tourism guide

These are the places locals will get annoyed you found out about. No marketing budget, no social media strategy, no hostess stand. Just good food made by people who've been doing it for decades in a city that keeps trying to gentrify them out.

Polk St · Cash Only
Swan Oyster Depot ⭐ (1517 Polk St)
Since 1912. An 18-seat marble counter that has not changed. The Sanicimino brothers. Fresh oysters, crab back, clam chowder, Dungeness crab — all sourced that morning. Bourdain called it "one of my favorite places to eat in America." Open Mon–Sat 8am–2:30pm only. CASH ONLY. No reservations. Arrive by 9am or expect a long wait. Closed Sunday.
Van Ness · SF Institution
House of Prime Rib
The most theatrical meal in SF. Whole roasts of prime rib carved tableside from silver carts, Yorkshire pudding, horseradish, spun salad, creamed spinach. Pure 1940s-San Francisco glamour. Bourdain lost his mind here over a martini and a half-pound slab of beef. Book weeks in advance — locals treat this as a sacred ritual.
Financial District · Since 1849
Tadich Grill
The oldest restaurant in California — since 1849. Hangtown Fry (oysters, bacon, eggs — invented here during the Gold Rush). Sand dabs, petrale sole, cioppino. Bourdain ate here. Dark wood paneling, counter seating, waiters who have worked there for 30 years. Nothing has changed. Everything is the point.
North Beach · Late Night
Original Joe's / Sam's Diner
Bourdain famously declared that after a long night drinking, all you need is Sam's in North Beach — double cheeseburger, fries, and a beer. "Anytime you begin to doubt the wonderfulness of San Francisco, really, all you gotta do is come here." Open until 3am. The cure for everything.
Embarcadero · Old Guard
Red's Java House (Pier 30)
A shack on the Embarcadero since 1955. Basic cheeseburger on sourdough, cheap beer, Bay Bridge views. Bourdain's final stop on No Reservations. The kind of place that exists because SF is still, in some corners, the SF of dockworkers and fishermen. Go for breakfast on a foggy morning.
Embarcadero
Pier 23 Café
All the bay views, none of the Fisherman's Wharf tourist infrastructure. A waterfront bar with outdoor seating right on the Embarcadero. Live music, local crowd, oysters and cold beer. Bourdain recommended it over Fisherman's Wharf every time he was in SF.
SoMa · BBQ
4505 Burgers & BBQ (Divisadero)
The Presidential Platter: brisket, smoked chicken, pork ribs, jalapeño cheddar hot links, chicharrones, coleslaw, baked beans, fries. Bourdain said: "Life is suddenly very, very good." A temple to American BBQ in the middle of SF. Outdoor seating, communal tables, smoke drifting across Divisadero.
Outer Sunset
Outerlands
The Outer Sunset's best-kept secret, a few blocks from Ocean Beach. Wood-fired sourdough, seasonal California small plates, excellent natural wine list. Homemade ricotta Dutch pancakes on weekend brunch. The kind of neighborhood restaurant that makes locals feel lucky they live where they live.
🥐 SF Bakeries — The Full Picture
Richmond · Croissant Pilgrimage
Arsicault Bakery ⭐
Bon Appétit named this the best croissant in America in 2016 and the line hasn't got shorter since. The morning bun (flaky croissant pastry, orange sugar, cinnamon) is the sleeper hit. Opens at 7am. Go early — they sell out by 10am most days. Inner Richmond, a neighborhood worth the journey.
Mission · Afternoon Only
Tartine Bakery ⭐ (18th & Guerrero)
The most influential bakery in America. Chad Robertson's country bread only comes out at 5pm — the entire afternoon queue forms for a $14 loaf with a crackle of crust and open, chewy crumb. Get there by 4:30pm. The croissants, morning buns, and croque monsieur in the morning are equally essential.
Mission
Butter & Crumble
The Instagrammable custom cake shop that somehow lives up to its press. Burnt Basque cheesecake, laminated pastries, custom creations using local California produce. One of the Mission's most beloved neighborhood bakeries — the kind of place that makes you linger.
North Beach · Since 1956
Café Trieste
SF's first espresso café, opened 1956 by Giovanni Giotta. Coppola wrote the Godfather screenplay here. Beat poets argued over their espressos here. Saturday afternoon opera — the staff and regulars sing Italian opera. Unchanged. Still the best argument that North Beach belongs to poets, not tourists.
Japantown · Since 1906
Benkyodo Mochi
The oldest Japanese-American confectionery in the US — survived the 1906 earthquake, survived internment, survived everything. Hand-rolled mochi and manju made using 120-year-old family recipes. Bubblegum, strawberry, sesame, red bean. Buy a box. The owners are descendants of the original family.
Various · Third Wave
Sightglass Coffee (SoMa)
SF's most serious coffee roaster in a massive, light-flooded SoMa space. The seasonal single-origin pourover program rivals anything in London's third-wave scene. Pastries from a rotating list of local bakers. If you take coffee seriously, this is where you come.
Chinatown
Good Mong Kok Bakery (Chinatown)
The hole-in-the-wall morning institution on Stockton Street. Egg tarts, pineapple buns, cocktail buns, taro puffs. $1-2 each. The line at 8am is entirely Chinese grandmothers — which is the best possible review. Cash only, no seating, absolutely essential.
Sunset District
Devil's Teeth Baking Company
The Outer Sunset's beloved neighborhood bakery. Breakfast sandwiches on house-baked biscuits, cinnamon rolls, exceptional coffee. A few blocks from Ocean Beach — the perfect start to a morning surf walk. Everything feels handmade because it is.

The Asian Diaspora Table
Filipino · Vietnamese · Chinese · Korean · Japanese · Indian · The Bay's greatest food inheritance

Why This Matters — A Note for CG & Bella

  • The Bay Area has the largest Filipino population outside the Philippines — San Francisco and Daly City are the epicentre
  • San Jose's Vietnamese community (Story Road) is one of the largest in the world outside Vietnam — the food there is extraordinary and completely undiscovered by tourists
  • The Richmond District (Clement Street) is what locals call the "second Chinatown" — more authentic, less touristy, better food
  • South Bay (Milpitas, Fremont, Cupertino) has the best Indian food outside India — a direct result of Silicon Valley's tech diaspora
  • None of this is on the tourist trail. All of it is accessible by car in under an hour.
🇵🇭 Filipino — The Bay's Heartbeat
North Beach · Elevated
Abacá
The finest Cal-Filipino restaurant in the city. Chef Francis Ang's menu in the Kimpton Buchanan Hotel reimagines kare-kare, sisig fried rice, and crispy pata with California produce and refinement. The Kamayan feast (banana leaf service, eaten with hands) is transformative. A landmark for Filipino food in America.
Mission · Fusion
Señor Sisig
The Filipino-Mexican fusion burrito that only makes sense in San Francisco. Pork sisig (crispy chopped pork with calamansi) wrapped in a Mission burrito. The tosilog crunchwrap is a cult item. The back patio on Valencia has local beer and a great vibe.
Daly City · Hole in the Wall
Tselogs
All-day Filipino breakfast institution in Daly City. Tapsilog (marinated beef, garlic rice, runny egg), longsilog, and bangsilog — the holy trinity of Filipino morning plates. $10 gets you more food than you can finish. Shrimp chips arrive automatically. Cash-friendly, very local.
Daly City · Gas Station Legend
Russ Fam Kitchen
Inside a Daly City gas station. The lechon kawali (slow-roasted then deep-fried pork belly) is so crispy that locals joke it could set off car alarms. Takeout-only. Also: outstanding lumpia and dinakdakan. This is the kind of place Bourdain would have loved and featured without explanation.
Excelsior · Cash Only
Kusina Ni Tess
Filipino comfort food served from a glass case, cafeteria-style. Pork adobo, kare-kare, dinuguan (pork blood stew — don't knock it), and laing (taro leaves in coconut milk). $6–$10 plates. Cash only, closes at 2pm. The real thing, no apologies.
SoMa · Modern
Mestiza
Outdoor-only Filipino fusion in SoMa with a plant-wall patio that's surprisingly beautiful on foggy nights (they have heaters). Elevated takes on sisig, kare-kare, and pancit with natural wine pairings. The kind of place that makes you realize Filipino food is having its moment.

Hole in the Wall

The Bourdain Principle

Anthony Bourdain spent four episodes in the Bay Area and almost never went to a famous restaurant. He ate at counter stools, gas station kitchens, and century-old diners. The places below embody that ethos — no ambiance, possibly terrible signage, and some of the most memorable food you will ever eat.

⭐ Bourdain Was Here

🦁 Sea Lions & Seals — SF Bay Area
Pier 39 · SF · Free · Year-round
Sea Lions at Pier 39
California sea lions arrived at Pier 39's K-dock spontaneously after the 1989 Loma Prieta earthquake and never left. Today up to 900 sea lions haul out, argue, bark, and sleep in the sun at the floating docks at the west side of Pier 39. Free viewing year-round. They are at their most numerous November–May. The Marine Mammal Center volunteers are usually present and can answer questions.
🦁 Best viewing early morning before the crowds — the sea lions are loudest and most active at dawn
Free
Point Reyes · Marin · 1.5 hrs
Point Reyes Elephant Seal Overlook
Chimney Rock at Point Reyes National Seashore has an elephant seal viewpoint with hundreds of seals hauled out on the beach below — you look down from a clifftop trail onto the colony. In April the young of the year are weaning. Also accessible: tule elk herds, harbor seals at Drakes Beach, and gray whales from the Point Reyes headlands.
🐘 Free · nps.gov/pore for maps · Combine with Hog Island Oyster Farm (both in Marin/Tomales Bay area)

Año Nuevo State Reserve — Elephant Seals
From April, the guided tour season transitions to self-guided access — walk among hundreds of Northern elephant seals resting on the beach. Males can weigh 2,000 lbs and look prehistoric up close. 🦦 Sea Otters — Monterey Bay
Moss Landing · 1.5 hrs south
Elkhorn Slough — Sea Otter Capital of California
The Elkhorn Slough National Estuarine Research Reserve near Moss Landing has the densest concentration of sea otters in California. In spring, mothers float on their backs with pups on their chests in the kelp. Kayak tours from Elkhorn Slough Safari run through the channels — you're eye-level with the otters, sea lions, and harbor seals. One of the most extraordinary wildlife experiences in the state.
🦦 Elkhorn Slough Safari: elkhornslough.com — 2-hour pontoon boat tours. Combine with Monterey Aquarium same day.

🐋 Whale Watching — April Migration
April Migration Season
Gray Whale Migration — Best Spots in April
Gray whales migrate north through April — heading from Baja California back to Alaska. Up to 26,000 gray whales pass the California coast. The best land-based watching spots: Bodega Head (Sonoma Coast), Point Reyes headlands (Chimney Rock trail), and the Monterey coast. Look for a grey spout and a dark rounded back rolling through the swells. Binoculars essential.
🐋 Best windows: calm mornings, early fog burn-off, from any coastal headland. Bodega Head on the Sonoma trip is perfectly positioned.
Seasonal
Boat Tours · 2–3 hrs
Princess Monterey / Sanctuary Cruises — Whale Watching Boats
Monterey Bay is one of the richest whale watching destinations in the world — the Monterey Bay Canyon (deepest canyon on the West Coast) concentrates krill, which feeds everything. April brings gray whales migrating north, and blue whales and humpbacks begin arriving in late April. Sanctuary Cruises (Moss Landing) and Princess Monterey (Fisherman's Wharf) both run 3-hour tours.
🚢 sanctuarycruises.com · Book morning departures — calmer seas and better wildlife activity

🌊 Sonoma Coast — Underrated Hidden Gems
Sonoma Coast State Park
Kortum Trail — Sonoma Coast Bluffs
The Kortum Trail runs 4.5 miles along the Sonoma Coast bluffs between Shell Beach and Blind Beach — one of the most dramatic and completely uncrowded coastal walks in California. No signs from the highway, no visitor facilities, no crowds. Just wild bluffs, pounding surf, sea stacks, and in April: wildflowers above the cliffs and gray whale spouts offshore.
🌊 Trailhead at Shell Beach on Hwy 1. Almost no one does this walk. It's extraordinary.
Hidden Gem
Jenner · Sonoma Coast
Goat Rock Beach — Harbor Seal Colony
At the mouth of the Russian River where it meets the Pacific, Goat Rock Beach has a large harbor seal colony on the sand bar year-round. In spring, pups are born here — you can watch from the beach at a respectful distance. The contrast of the river mouth and the crashing Pacific is beautiful. Very few people come here.
🦭 Park at the Goat Rock lot. Walk north along the beach — the seals are on the sandbar where the river meets the ocean.
Hidden Gem
Salt Point · Sonoma Coast
Salt Point State Park — Tidepool Foraging + Abalone
Salt Point's rocky intertidal zone is one of the most biodiverse tide pool environments on the California coast — sea stars, anemones, urchins, chitons, limpets, and in the kelp beds: massive schools of rockfish and lingcod. The park was the site of intensive abalone harvesting until 2018 when populations collapsed. Walking the rocks at low tide is an extraordinary natural history experience.
🐠 Check tide tables before visiting — best tide pools at -1.0 ft or lower · Also the best mushroom foraging in Sonoma (see Sonoma tab)
Hidden Gem
Bodega Bay to Jenner
The 17-Mile Stretch — Sonoma's Most Underrated Drive
The coastal stretch from Bodega Bay north through Salmon Creek Beach, Gleason Beach, Arched Rock, Shell Beach, Goat Rock, and into Jenner at the Russian River is 17 miles of continuous coastal drama. Every pullout has a different landscape — sea stacks, empty sandy coves, crashing bluffs. Stop at every single one. Most are completely empty even on spring weekends.
🌊 Salmon Creek Beach: 2-mile crescent of sand with consistent waves and almost no people. Arched Rock: a natural sea arch visible from a pullout. No hike required.
